The Inspiring Journey of OSIM Foundation in Tanzania

OSIM Foundation, established in 2018, is committed to bolstering maternal and child health while championing initiatives for sanitary pad distribution, nutritional support, and the empowerment of women across remote communities within the Arusha region of Tanzania.

At its core, the organization prioritizes health education, fostering proactive health-seeking behaviors, and tackling poverty from various angles through socio-economic empowerment initiatives tailored for marginalized areas

Meet Pendo, a Maasai girl and the visionary behind OSIM Foundation. Her journey began with a master's research project focusing on birth preparedness and complication readiness among pregnant women in the underserved communities of Longido District, Arusha, Tanzania.

Through her research, Pendo uncovered alarming rates of maternal and under-five mortalities attributed to inadequate access to health facilities. Pregnant women were compelled to embark on long, arduous journeys on foot, often resulting in home deliveries or reliance on traditional birth attendants due to the absence of nearby medical assistance.

Having transcended cultural barriers herself, Pendo intimately understands the struggles faced by women and girls within the Maasai community. Here, the pursuit of education for girls is frequently overshadowed by early marriage practices.

During her pursuit of a master's degree in public health, Pendo forged a collaboration with Melfard Sorell, an American deeply moved by the challenges confronting young girls within Maasai families, particularly in the realm of menstrual hygiene management.

The absence of proper hygiene materials often led to girls dropping out of school, burdened by the shame of makeshift solutions. In response, Melfard Sorell resolved to sponsor and facilitate the provision of sanitary pads, undergarments, and soap for these girls.

Their partnership extended beyond addressing menstrual hygiene needs, venturing into various projects aimed at serving marginalized women in remote regions. Their collective mission? To usher in multidimensional health improvements and instill hope for a brighter future within these communities.
